BUTTONS

IHTSDO

INTERACTION

The manifestation of call to action UI elements in the digital space come in the form of clickable buttons. Buttons are used as a primary call to action, such as approving or accepting a request.

WHAT ARE THEY FOR?

The result of the button's action varies according to how it has been assigned. When clicked, buttons perform their action. e.g. 'Submit' or 'Next', that link to a page with additional information, a subsequent step in a process or feedback message.

 

They have three states: enabled, disabled and rollover.

BEHAVIOUR

Mandatorily, all buttons should contain a text label that clearly communicates its purpose or action. Some buttons may benefit from simple icons to accompany their text label. For example, adding a forward or backward arrow. These icons should sit alongside the line of copy and be centred horizontally in the button. In general, the icon should sit to the right of the button unless it’s a back button. Remember to allow sufficient clear space for the icon and retain a central, vertical alignment.

 

Labels should be kept short and clear, but a button's width can extend to accommodate lengthier labels.

 

Buttons should be employed when a user is required to submit or approve their selection or input as part of any process or view.

 

Button placement, alignment and arrangement are essential factors to observe in order to instil a consistent experience.

HOW AND WHEN TO USE

Placement of CTA buttons on-page and in modules is always bottom right. This is to support the logical flow of a user's eye movement from left to right, top to bottom.

Selecting which size button to use is an important consideration. There are three sizes to choose from. The size should be relative to its surrounding elements or reflective of the importance of content/task. Never use secondary buttons that are lost amongst a dense page of content. Espcially when they are integral to the actions of that page.

Primary

Used for most actions. Especially those as a part of forms. Having a primary button clearly indicates to the user the most important action on the page. If more than one primary button is required, it might be worth considering splitting the task over two pages.

Secondary

Used for secondary/support and minor actions.

Oversized

This oversized button should be used as part of a work flow such as that within mapping, when assigning work from one user to another. For example, it would display a left or right arrow together with the label 'Assign' or 'Return'. The oversized button should never be used as a primary call to action (forms).

Grouped Toggle

These buttons work conjointly, meaning they function effectively as radio buttons. They can be used as a selection method to toggle between pairs or selection of options such as a search parameter type.

The order of a button arrangement is always left to right, from negative to positive (e.g. 'Decline / Accept' or 'Back / Next'). This is because semiotically, they suggest a movement backward or forward. The buttons should always be aligned on the same horizontal axis.

EXAMPLES

Never use a button where a Hyperlink will suffice. For example among body copy or as support, non-primary navigation.

 

Never use a rogue colour or treatment outside of the IHTSDO brand guidelines.
